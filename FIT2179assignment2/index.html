Where Do Australians Spend Their Money?

Australians' spending patterns reveal how lifestyle, cost of living, and geography shape financial behaviour across the country. This interactive story explores where household budgets go — from daily essentials to discretionary pleasures — and how these patterns differ by income, location, and household type.

Data sources: Australian Bureau of Statistics (ABS) & CEIC Data. Figures in AUD millions unless otherwise stated.

Section 1: Australians' Spending Landscape

The Big Picture

Australian households collectively spend over $80 billion per month. Transport leads at $14.0 billion, closely followed by Recreation & Culture ($13.4B) and Food ($12.6B). Housing — while not always captured as a standalone industry in this dataset — represents the largest structural burden when considered across rent, mortgages, and utilities.

💡 How to read this chart: Hover over each bar to see exact spending figures. Bars are sorted from highest to lowest spending category. Colours are consistent across all charts in this story.

Spending Structure

The donut chart reveals the proportional share of each category. Transport, Recreation, and Food together account for nearly 47% of all household spending. Health and Hotels/Cafes/Restaurants each represent significant shares, reflecting Australia's service-oriented economy and lifestyle priorities.

💡 How to read this chart: Each coloured segment represents a spending category's share of the total. Hover to see the exact percentage and dollar amount. The same colour is used for each category across all visualisations.

Key Insight

Transport and Recreation together make up over one-third of total spending, highlighting the importance of mobility and lifestyle in Australian household budgets.

Section 2: Housing & Essentials — The Cost of Living

Housing Dominates

When housing is considered across all its forms (rent, mortgage, utilities, maintenance), it emerges as the single largest spending category for Australian households. The bar chart below starkly contrasts Housing against all other categories combined.

■ Housing is consistently represented in deep blue (#1f4e79) throughout this visualisation to maintain colour constancy. ■ Food is always orange, ■ Transport is always purple, and so on.

💡 User Guide: Notice how Housing (deep blue) stands alone on the left bar, while all other categories are grouped in grey on the right. This emphasises the scale of housing costs.

Essential vs Non-Essential

Not all spending is discretionary. Essential categories — those households cannot easily cut back on — include Food, Transport, Health, and Housing. Together, these essential categories represent a significant portion of total outgoings, leaving limited room for financial flexibility.

The grouped bar chart compares Essential (deep blue) and Non-Essential (orange) spending across major aggregated categories. We intentionally avoid using red and green together to ensure accessibility for colour-blind users.

Key Insight

Essential spending on Food, Housing, and Transport alone ($52.1B) exceeds all non-essential categories combined, illustrating the rigidity of modern household budgets.

Section 3: Income Differences — Who Feels the Squeeze?

Spending Flow by Income

Australian households spend money very differently depending on their income level (left). While housing remains the dominant expense for everyone, its relative financial burden is significantly higher for lower-income groups, reflecting the intense cost-of-living pressures currently affecting many Australians.

This diagram visualises how expenditure flows from income groups (left) to spending categories (middle) and finally into essential and non-essential spending (right). The width of each band encodes proportional spending volume, allowing viewers to compare financial priorities across income levels. To maintain visual clarity, we have grouped the Entertainment, Clothing and Tourism items under the "Other" category.

💡 How to read this Sankey diagram: Follow the coloured bands from left to right. The left column shows three income groups; the middle shows four spending categories; the right separates spending into Essential vs Non-Essential. Hover over any band to see exact flow values. Band colour indicates the spending category.

The Housing Burden

Housing affordability is not distributed equally. Low-income Australians face a housing burden more than twice as heavy as high-income earners. This disparity has profound implications for financial stress, savings capacity, and quality of life.

The bar chart quantifies this inequality. While high-income households can allocate nearly 50% of their budget to discretionary spending, low-income households must dedicate over 80% to essentials.

Key Insight

Low-income households spend 38.2% on Housing vs 18.3% for high-income — a gap of nearly 20 percentage points that shapes financial inequality across Australia.

Section 4: Geography — Where You Live Matters

Housing Pressure by Region

The choropleth map below uses a blue colour scale to show housing cost burden across Australian states and territories. Darker shades indicate higher proportions of income devoted to housing.

New South Wales bears the heaviest housing burden at 35.9%, followed closely by Victoria at 34.2%. At the other end, the Northern Territory has the lowest burden at 27.3%.

💡 How to read this map: Darker blue = higher housing burden. Hover over each state to see exact percentages, total spending, and population data. This map uses a Mercator projection centred on Australia.

Spending Scale Across the Nation

The proportional symbol map overlays circle size (total spending) and colour intensity (housing burden) on a geographic base. Larger circles reflect higher absolute spending — naturally concentrated in populous states like NSW and Victoria.

Interestingly, the orange colour scale for housing burden reveals that higher total spending does not always mean higher housing pressure. Western Australia, despite significant spending, maintains a relatively moderate housing burden of 29.5%.

Key Insight

NSW and Victoria combine the highest absolute spending with the heaviest housing burdens, creating a "double pressure" effect for residents in Australia's most populous states.

Section 5: Lifestyle & Change — How Spending Evolves

Spending Trends Over Time

This interactive line chart tracks spending from 2019 to 2026. Use the dropdown menu above the chart to filter by category, or select "Show All Categories" to see every trend simultaneously.

The chart reveals a clear post-COVID recovery: after the 2020 dip, spending across most categories has rebounded strongly. By 2026, Transport reached $14.6B and Recreation hit $14.1B, reflecting continued lifestyle momentum despite cost-of-living pressures.

💡 Interactive Guide: Use the "Select Category" dropdown to isolate specific trends. Hover over data points to see exact spending amounts for each year. The coloured lines use a consistent palette matching other charts in this story.

Transport vs Recreation Shift

The area chart focuses on two categories that illustrate changing lifestyles: Transport (purple) and Recreation & Culture (amber). Both have grown steadily since 2020, extending into 2026 with Transport at $14.6B and Recreation at $14.1B.

Transport spending has shown the steepest growth, driven by fuel costs and renewed commuting. Recreation spending, while also growing, reflects a more gradual return to pre-pandemic entertainment and cultural activities.

Key Insight

By 2026, Transport spending ($14.6B) has grown faster than Recreation ($14.1B) since 2022, suggesting that cost-of-living pressures (fuel, vehicle costs) are outpacing lifestyle recovery.

Section 6: Household Type Insights

Families, Singles, and Retirees

Different household structures allocate their budgets in markedly different ways. Families face the highest absolute housing costs, while retirees spend proportionally more on Health. Singles, with lower total outgoings, enjoy relatively more flexibility in discretionary categories like Recreation.

The grouped bar chart compares weekly spending across five core categories for three household types. Housing (deep blue) remains the dominant expense for all groups, but its relative weight varies significantly.

💡 How to read this chart: Each cluster of bars represents a household type. Within each cluster, coloured bars show spending by category. Hover for exact weekly dollar amounts.

Key Insight

Retirees spend the highest proportion on Health ($350/week), while Families face the steepest Housing costs ($850/week) — reflecting different life-stage priorities and pressures.

Spending Profile Radar

The radar chart offers a holistic view of how Families (deep blue), Singles (orange), and Retirees (purple) distribute their budgets across six key dimensions. Each axis represents a spending category, with distance from the centre indicating relative spending intensity (normalised to the maximum in each category).

Families show the most "balanced" large-scale profile, dominating Housing, Food, and Transport. Retirees stand out in Health and Recreation. Singles display a more compact profile across all categories, reflecting lower absolute outgoings.

💡 How to read this radar chart: Each axis represents a spending category. The further a polygon vertex is from the centre, the higher that household type's relative spending in that category. Compare the three overlapping shapes to see lifestyle differences. Hover over data points for exact normalised values.

Key Insight

Retirees' Health spending is their strongest relative category (100% normalised), while Singles' profile is the most evenly distributed — a signature of leaner, more flexible budgeting.
